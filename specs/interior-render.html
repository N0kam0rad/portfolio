Interior Render Studio

An AI interior-design tool — point it at your room, pick a style, and get photorealistic renders of what it could become.

PrototypeAI image genDesign toolReact
What it is

The 10-second version

A studio where you choose a room type and a style (maximalist, boho, modern, dopamine decor, industrial, Scandinavian), feed it your space plus a detailed prompt (dimensions, orientation, lighting), and it generates photorealistic interior renders you can iterate on.

Why I built it

The spark

Picturing a redesign in your head is hard, and hiring a designer to mock it up is expensive. I wanted to see the room transformed before moving a single piece of furniture.

The problem it solved for me

Decide before you spend

Test wildly different styles on my actual space in seconds, so design choices come from seeing real options instead of guessing — and committing budget with confidence.

The problem it solves for a user

Homeowners, renters, realtors, stagers

Anyone making a space decision: visualize a remodel, virtually stage a listing, or sell a client on a direction. Interior visualization is a hot, paid market — this makes it instant and cheap.

How it works

Under the hood

A React app with curated room and style presets and reference imagery. You upload a photo and a richly structured prompt — exact room dimensions, an orientation lock so the AI doesn't rearchitect the space, lighting and material direction — and it returns photorealistic renders to compare and refine.

The business model

  • Credits / subscription — pay per render or a monthly tier for unlimited.
  • Pro tier — realtors and stagers get batch renders and branded exports.
  • Shoppable renders — affiliate links to the furniture and decor shown in each result.

Next: tighter room-fidelity controls and a "shop this look" layer.

What I learned building it

How I directed the AI

Constrain the room, free the style

The trick was a structured prompt that fixed the things that must not change (dimensions, layout, orientation) while leaving style wide open. Telling the model what to preserve mattered more than telling it what to create.

What broke & how I fixed it

It redesigned the architecture

Left to its own devices, the model moved windows and walls — pretty renders of the wrong room. The fix was explicit "do not alter" orientation locks in the prompt, so the redesign stays your room, just restyled.

The automation win

A reusable prompt scaffold

Style presets plus a fixed prompt template mean a non-expert gets gallery-quality results without writing a paragraph of prompt engineering. The hard part is pre-built; the user just picks.

Tools that did the work

Wrap the model in a UI

The real product value wasn't the image model — it's the React interface and the prompt scaffolding around it that make it usable. Lesson: the wrapper is the product.

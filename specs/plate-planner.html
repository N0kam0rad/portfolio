Product Spec · App 05

My Plate Planner

A meal-planner that actually respects your dietary needs — filters by what you can eat, tells you what to cook from your fridge, and tracks protein without the obsession.

BuiltMobile appLocal-firstDiet-aware
What it is

The 10-second version

A meal-planning and grocery app built around a real dietary profile (halal/kosher, sensitivities like onion-free, foods you avoid). It generates a per-store weekly grocery list with quantities, suggests recipes from ingredients you already have, runs meal-prep checklists, and keeps a gentle protein tally.

Why I built it

The spark

Generic meal plans ignore the constraints that actually matter — allergies, sensitivities, halal/kosher, things you just won't eat. I wanted a planner that started from my rules and never broke them.

The problem it solved for me

A plan I could actually follow

One place that turned a high-protein goal plus a long list of restrictions into a real weekly menu, a sorted grocery run, and a prep schedule — no spreadsheets, no re-checking every label.

The problem it solves for a user

Anyone with food rules

Allergies, religious requirements, picky eaters, macro goals — a huge audience underserved by one-size-fits-all apps. This filters everything through their real constraints automatically.

How it works

Under the hood

A dietary-profile setup drives the whole app. A smart grocery list groups items by store with weekly quantities; a "What Can I Make?" fridge mode suggests recipes from what you have; a meal tracker shows a simple protein bar; meal-prep checklists with timers; and a supplement schedule. Local-first, no account needed.

Where it's going · how it monetizes

The business model

  • Freemium — core planning free; advanced filters, multi-profile households, and macro tracking as a subscription.
  • Grocery affiliate — one-tap carts to delivery services (Shipt, Amazon Fresh, Instacart).
  • Creator packs — dietitian- and creator-made recipe/meal-plan bundles sold in-app.

Next: a real recipe database, the ready-to-paste store carts, and reminder notifications.

What I learned building it

How I directed the AI

Constraints before recipes

I told the AI to treat the dietary profile as the foundation — every recipe and grocery item filters through it — rather than generating a generic plan and bolting restrictions on after. Designing from the constraint outward made the "What Can I Make?" mode the standout feature.

What broke & how I fixed it

It kept ignoring my rules

Early drafts cheerfully suggested onion in a recipe I'd flagged onion-free. Lesson: an AI will drift past a stated rule unless the rule is structural. I had it bake sensitivities into the data model and swap offending ingredients (onion → capers) automatically, not just mention them.

The automation win

Plan → grocery list, automatically

The weekly menu generates the shopping list with quantities, grouped by the exact stores I use. The boring part — "what do I actually need to buy, and where" — happens on its own from the plan.

Tools that did the work

Claude to plan, Gemini to build

I used Claude to nail the plan, product spec, and the exact build prompt, then handed that prompt to Gemini Canvas to scaffold the app. Right tool for each job: one for thinking, one for generating the UI.

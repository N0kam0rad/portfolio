Build Log · AestheticSim

How I built the medspa OS.

By Tamara Richards·Served
React Gemini Prompt design Two-sided workflow

AestheticSim turns a subjective medspa consultation into a structured, clinically-grounded plan — a real on-device skin scan, an identity-preserving treatment simulation, and a downloadable board a client actually wants to keep.

The problem

Medspa consultations are expensive for the client and high-pressure for the clinician. Most of the time, the client leaves with a verbal explanation they can barely remember and no visual proof of what was discussed. The clinician has to rebuild the same conversation from scratch every time. That gap was the spark.

AestheticSim is a two-sided workflow: the client completes an intake with real on-device photo analysis, the clinician reviews an auto-generated starter plan against that scan, and the approved result lands back on the client's dashboard — plus a downloadable board they take home.

Key flows built

  • Real on-device skin scan: camera or upload, 478-point facial landmarking (MediaPipe), 7 skin-concern scores, a tappable heatmap — nothing leaves the device
  • Identity-preserving facial-balancing simulation: a landmark-driven, depth-aware warp calibrated to real clinical units, with a VECTRA-style contour/volume-difference mode
  • Unified intake → review → approval loop: the 4-photo intake runs the real scan and routes straight into the provider's review queue with an auto-generated starter plan — no more separate mocked "analyzing" state
  • Provider plan customization: an Injectables/Facials/Both filter, a treatment-library dropdown, a custom-treatment form (requires a procedure description + benefits), and recommended products with sponsored/partner badges
  • Provider governance: a dose-visibility toggle and a clinic-level kill switch that can disable the entire simulation for jurisdictions that regulate simulated before/afters
  • Downloadable consultation board: executive summary, facial analysis, skin quality, treatment phases, cost forecast
The strongest demo path: from the landing screen, run "Skin Analysis — Live Scan" with your own photo, then toggle a treatment on the simulator to see the before/after shift. That's the one sequence that shows the full value in under two minutes.

Clinical grounding & research

A simulation that invents numbers is worse than no simulation — it sets wrong expectations. So the dosing, the treatment longevity, and the simulation physics are all grounded in published ranges, encoded in a single CLINICAL_REFERENCE constant in the app so the figures can't drift between the plan, the units, and the on-photo simulation. The idea was sparked by a study using 3D-printed facial models to train injectors — anatomy-first, not vibes-first.

Units per area

  • Neuromodulator (Botox): glabella 15–25 U · forehead 10–20 U (label 20 U) · crow's feet ~24 U · masseter 40–60 U total. Onset 3–7 days, peak ~2 wks, lasts 3–4 mo. It relaxes muscle and lifts — it adds no volume. Dosing · mechanism
  • HA filler: cheek 1–2 mL · tear trough 0.5–1 mL · lips ~1 mL · chin 1–2 mL · jawline 2–4 mL (~4 mL/session, ~1:1 volume). Lasts 6–18 mo by area. MD Codes · longevity (MRI)
  • Biostimulator (Sculptra): 2–4 vials, ~1 vial/decade; collagen builds over 8–12 wks; ~2-yr result — no instant fill. Sculptra
  • Regenerative / energy: PRF for under-eyes (results 2–6 wks on); RF microneedling collagen peaks 6–12 wks, lasts 12–18 mo. PRF · RF microneedling

Why the simulation physics differ by treatment

The on-photo simulator runs a landmark-driven warp, but each treatment behaves the way it does clinically: filler adds outward volume (scaled by mL), neuromodulator lifts the brow and softens lines or slims the masseter (never a balloon effect), biostimulator volumizes gradually, and energy/skincare is a tone-and-glow pass. Injection points are anchored to the real MD-Code zones and colour-coded by treatment type.

The 3D model

The scan runs entirely on-device via Google's MediaPipe Face Landmarker — 478 3D landmarks (with depth), blendshapes, and a facial transformation matrix — so the warp is depth-aware and the injection zones sit on a real face mesh. This mirrors how clinical tools like Crisalix and Canfield VECTRA XT (stereophotogrammetry, slider-based "Face Sculptor" for injectables) work — and the research matters: simulation accuracy correlates with patient satisfaction (r = 0.66), which is exactly why the app frames every render as an approximate planning visual, not a promise.

VECTRA XT's interface includes a "Gray Mode" and volume-difference mapping so a provider can read shape change independent of skin tone and lighting. The simulator borrows that directly: a contour-map mode desaturates the "after" photo and overlays a heat-style shading — warm where a treatment adds volume (filler, biostimulator), cool where it lifts or displaces tissue (neuromodulator, PRF) — so the 3D effect reads clearly even on a single photo.

3D approaches evaluated but not integrated

Two more sophisticated options were researched and deliberately set aside, for reasons worth being honest about:

  • FLAME is the academic gold-standard parametric 3D head model (shape, pose, and expression learned from 33,000+ scans), with single-image reconstruction research (DECA, EMOCA, MICA). It's license-gated (signup + model license required) and those reconstruction tools are Python/PyTorch research code — not something that drops into a static, client-side React app.
  • KeenTools produces excellent textured 3D head reconstructions, but it needs multiple photos from different angles (not the single front-facing photo this flow captures), runs as a paid cloud API or a Blender/Nuke/Houdini plugin, and has no browser path.

Given a single photo, no backend, and free hosting on GitHub Pages, the MediaPipe landmark-warp + contour-map approach is the pragmatic match — not a downgrade from FLAME/KeenTools, a different point on the cost/fidelity curve.

Safety

The intake's medical flags map to the real high-risk picture: the nose, glabella, tear trough, and nasolabial fold are the vascular danger zones where occlusion (≈1/5,000 syringes) can cause necrosis or vision loss — managed with prompt hyaluronidase. The same complications are exactly what professional simulators like ACCURATE's BeautySim (VR + haptic-feedback syringe) train injectors to recognize early. That's why a planning tool should inform the consult, never replace the clinician.

Every number in AestheticSim traces to a citation above. A provider toggle controls whether clients see exact doses or just treatment names — and a separate clinic-level switch can turn the entire simulation off (some jurisdictions regulate digitally-simulated before/afters in medical advertising), leaving the skin-analysis and treatment plan untouched.

What comes next

No paying customers or confirmed demand yet — that's the honest current state, and the actual next step: real conversations with independent medspa owners and injectors before any more engineering. If that validates, Phase 2 is a real backend (auth, billing, persistence via Supabase) and a formal compliance/legal review before any real patient use.

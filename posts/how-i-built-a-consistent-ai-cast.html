How I built a perfectly consistent AI cast

By Tamara Richards·~8 min read
LLM scriptingImage generationReference elementsCanon bibleExpression packsCapCut

The hardest part of making an AI film isn't getting one good frame. It's getting the same character — same face, same bonnet, same braids — to show up in frame number three hundred, furious in one shot and sobbing in the next. Here's the exact system I use to keep a whole cast on-model across an entire series.

One character — Tamara — three moods, zero drift. Same braids, same bonnet, same nose studs every time.

The project

BonnetNoona is a faceless claymation comedy series about sisters trying to build an influencer brand and gloriously failing at it. Soft puffy 3D clay, warm mockumentary lighting, shot vertical for TikTok. The whole thing lives or dies on one question: do the characters stay themselves from shot to shot?

Early on, they didn't. Skin tones drifted. Bonnets changed colour. Tamara would walk into the next shot with a different nose and lose her braids entirely. So I stopped trusting the prompt to remember things and built a system that does the remembering for me. It comes down to five moves.

The one trick: stop describing your characters and start referencing them. A saved reference element plus a locked canon doc beats a perfect prompt every single time.

The relationship map matters as much as the faces

Consistency isn't only "same face twice." For a series to hold together, each regular has to stay themselves across jokes, rooms, reactions, and chaos — which means locking far more than a look. For every character I lock:

  • Visual identity — the exact bonnet, braids, skin tone, and scale.
  • Personality — the default face and the energy they bring to a room.
  • Family relationships — who narrates, who escalates, who keeps the peace.
  • Pet relationships — Koko & Glenn are brothers; Peppa & Shadow are siblings; Shadow & Glenn are best friends.
  • Scene behavior — what they actually do when the camera lands on them.
  • Expression packs — a fixed set of reusable faces per character.
  • Repeatable reference IDs — so any of the above gets summoned, never re-described.

That's the difference between "I generated some cute characters" and a production bible for an AI-directed series — a repeatable system, not a lucky batch.

01  Write the canon before you generate anything

Before a single image, I write a canon bible — the one source of truth for the whole series. It locks the stuff a model loves to drift on: each character's exact colour, skin tone, scale relative to the others, personality, and the things that must never happen.

A few real lines from mine:

  • Tamara — deep purple satin bonnet, long auburn box-braids past the shoulder, gold hoops, two gold nose studs, purple neck-scarf, cream off-shoulder top. Warm brown skin. Big-sister warmth.
  • Kalila — black bonnet with a hot-pink ruffle trim and a music-note print, pink-and-white striped pajamas, nose ring; medium-brown skin, do not darken. Resting expression is unbothered, never sweet.
  • Koko — WHITE curly poodle, PINK ears, knee-height — not brown, not tan, not a lapdog.

Those "nots" are doing real work. Half of consistency is just telling the model what to stop guessing.

02  Lock each character as a reusable reference

This is the part that actually solves consistency. Each character gets generated once as a clean reference, then saved as a reusable element with its own ID. From then on I never re-describe a face — I summon them by reference, so the bonnet, braids, skin tone, and proportions come back identical every time.

In the prompt it looks like this — drop the element ID in, then describe only the action:

<<<tamara-element-id>>> Tamara in her deep purple satin bonnet,
auburn braids down, striding in mid-direction with one finger up,
holding Koko's leash. Big-sister energy. Cozy living room,
handheld mockumentary framing. [ + global style block ]

The payoff: one locked character, every mood

Once Tamara is a saved reference, I can throw any emotion at her and she stays unmistakably herself. This is an expression pack — the same locked character, generated fresh for each beat. I build one per main character so I've got a face ready for every joke before I ever start a scene.

…and she survives a total style swap

The real stress test: keep the character locked, then change the entire art style. Same Tamara — same bonnet, braids, hoops, nose studs, the hand-to-cheek smirk — rendered four completely different ways. If the reference is doing its job, she's recognisable in all of them.

BonnetNoona ships in the clay style on the left — the rest are stress tests. But proving she holds across styles is how I know the reference is locked tight enough to trust across hundreds of shots.

03  Paste the same style block into every shot

Consistency isn't just faces — it's the look. I keep one global style block and paste it, unchanged, into every single still. It carries the medium, the lighting, and a long list of "no"s:

Soft puffy 3D claymation, matte clay surfaces, rounded forms,
satin bonnets, cozy interior, warm mockumentary lighting.
Warm brown matte clay skin, uniform tone per character.
No flat emoji/vector, no photoreal, no glam, no harsh caricature. 9:16.

Boring? Yes. But the model has no memory between generations — the style block is its memory.

04  Build a scene shot-by-shot, not all at once

Here's where the system earns its keep. I never try to generate a whole scene in one go. I break it into shots, reuse the same reference IDs across every one, pull the right face from the expression pack, and only animate the beats where movement carries the joke.

Lock the set, too

Characters aren't the only thing that drifts — rooms do, too. So each sister's space gets locked the same way: I build the room once, then save it as an environment reference at a few fixed camera angles. Here's Kalila's room — same space, three locked views, ready for a character to drop into.

Then build the scene shot by shot

With the cast and the sets both locked, a scene is just assembly — drop the right character (and the right face from the expression pack) into the right room, one shot at a time. Below is one scene — the "Koko drags Tamara out of the shot" bit — built the way I actually build it.

Scene · "The interview that didn't happen"

Three shots, one consistent cast

Shot 1 — stillTamara mid-sentence to camera, Koko sitting calm at her feet. Slow push-in.
Refs: tamara + koko. Held still — the comedy is the calm before.
Shot 2 — motionKoko spots something off-frame and bolts; leash yanks. This one gets animated.
Same two refs, motion model. The only animated shot in the scene.
Shot 3 — stillEmpty chair, swinging mic, Kalila leans in unbothered: "Anyway."
Ref: kalila. Punchline lands on a still.

Drop your exported stills/clips into the three slots above. Because all three pull the same locked references, the cast stays identical even though the shots were generated separately.

When you're stamping the buttons later, the captions ride these beats:

PRODUCER MODE: ACTIVATEDTHE QUEEN DECLINESANYWAY.

05  Assemble and stamp the buttons

Final assembly happens in CapCut: stills and clips on a timeline at ~2.5–3s each, caption "buttons" dropped on-beat, snap zooms on the talking beats, and a freeze-frame end card. The references and expression packs did the heavy lifting upstream, so the edit is just rhythm.

What I'd tell anyone starting

You do not need to be technical. You need a clear vision and the patience to write your "nots" down once. The reference-element trick is the whole game — lock your characters, build an expression pack for each, reuse them relentlessly, and let the canon doc be the memory the model doesn't have.
